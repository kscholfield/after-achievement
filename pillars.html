The Pillars

Five domains. No finish line.

From The Long View, Chapter 5

Most people who have spent time thinking about how they live have some version of a pillar framework — life categories scored on a wheel, domains ranked by how well they're being tended, areas of focus defined in a workshop or a book. The concept isn't new. What I hadn't thought carefully about until this season is a distinction that turns out to matter quite a bit: the difference between the pillar itself and the practices that express it.

Pillars are the permanent domains — the categories of what matters that don't change regardless of what life looks like. They aren't goals. Goals are directional; they aim toward completion. Pillars are stabilizing; they support continuity. In the first game, you could often run on goals because the structure around you was maintaining the pillars passively. Work imposed a schedule that enforced health by necessity. Relationships were sustained through shared life infrastructure. The pillar was always there; it just didn't require active tending.

When that structure disappears, the passive enforcement disappears with it. The second game doesn't introduce the concept of pillars. It reveals which ones were being maintained by default — and which ones now have to be chosen deliberately.

One more thing before getting into each pillar: these aren't things you tend equally every day. They're not a checklist to complete before going to sleep. They're the domains you keep from going dark — and which ones need more attention in a given season will shift. The goal is continuity, not perfection.

Pillar 01

Health

This is the most obvious pillar and the easiest to neglect when structure disappears. In the first game, health fit around everything else. Work dictated the schedule. Training happened when time allowed. But over time, health isn't something to be managed around life. It's the foundation that allows everything else.

For those who maintain stable routines after exit, this pillar often takes care of itself. The challenge comes with disruption. On the road, I no longer assume access to a familiar gym, preferred equipment, or a reliable time window. What I've found is that the anchor here isn't the workout itself — it's the decision to train. Frequency and identity, not optimization. Some days that means a crowded hotel gym. Other days it means walking, bodyweight work, or doing less than planned. The standard shifts from "am I training the way I want to?" to "am I someone who trains?"

Eating well follows the same logic. "Good enough, consistently" has proven more reliable than any ideal plan. Without health as a non-negotiable anchor, everything else becomes negotiable in ways that compound quietly.

Pillar 02

Relationships

In the absence of constant work demands, relationships take on a different role. They are no longer something to be fit into the margins. They become central.

This is especially true in a long marriage. Time together increases dramatically. Space opens up. And with that comes a different kind of attention — one that the busyness of the first game made easy to defer. Shirley and I have had to figure out things about each other that more than two decades of shared purpose had kept conveniently obscured.

This pillar isn't about scheduling quality time or being intentional in a performative sense. It's simpler than that — it's about paying attention. Noticing when the other person is running low on something. Noticing when you are. The same applies, differently, to the relationships that matter outside the marriage. Without the automatic structure of shared work or proximity, relationships have to be chosen. Which ones deserve that active investment is a question worth asking directly rather than letting inertia answer it.

Pillar 03

Wealth

In the first game, wealth was a problem of accumulation. Build more. Earn more. Reinvest. The direction was clear and the measure was obvious. In the second game, the problem changes entirely. Accumulation is no longer the goal. Stewardship is. The question shifts from how do I build this? to how do I manage what I have, allocate it wisely, and make sure it serves the life I'm trying to build?

There's a version of financial anxiety that gets quieter after you sell a business and another version that gets louder. The first is the fear of not having enough. The second is the fear of spending what you built. Both are worth naming, because neither goes away on its own.

What has helped is replacing vague vigilance with a small number of clear decisions. Knowing the actual number — what this life costs, what rate of spending is sustainable, what margin exists — is more calming than any amount of general financial diligence. Precision settles what anxiety can't. The anchor is a regular, honest review: are we on track, is the allocation aligned with how we actually want to live, and is money doing the work it's supposed to do?

Pillar 04

Legacy

Without the pressure to produce, thinking takes on a different quality. It slows down. Becomes more reflective. Less about solving immediate problems and more about understanding patterns, connections, and meaning. Writing has become the primary way I work through this — not as output, but as clarification.

But the deeper question isn't personal. It's what gets transmitted. Legacy isn't what happens after you're gone. It's what you actively build while you're here — what you choose to pass on, in what form, to whom, and with what intention. The writing matters. The conversations matter. The letters matter. Not because they ensure a particular outcome, but because they make the transmission deliberate rather than accidental.

For most of the first game, this pillar is essentially unfunded. There is no time, and there is always something more pressing. The second game is when it finally becomes possible to do it properly — which means starting, even when the form isn't clear yet.

Pillar 05

Energy and Attention

Of all the pillars, this one surprised me most. In the first game, energy was spent without much thought. There was always something that required it, so it was given. The feedback loop was short: you spent energy, something got done, you recovered and spent more. The work imposed its own rhythm, and attention followed automatically.

In the second game, both disappear as defaults. And without them, I've started to notice things I never tracked before: what gives energy back, what drains it without obvious return, what activities leave me feeling better than when I started versus which leave me vaguely depleted even when they were technically enjoyable.

Attention is the companion problem — and in some ways the more important one. Energy is the fuel; attention is how you allocate it. Left to default, attention fragments. Protected deliberately, it goes toward what actually matters. The difference between a day that feels purposeful and one that simply passes is usually less about what happened and more about where attention went. What protects both is space — unscheduled time, uncommitted days, room to think without agenda. In the first game, an open afternoon felt like a small failure of productivity. In the second game, space becomes something to protect.

The Point

Freedom creates space.
Anchors determine what fills it.
